Gun Violence in America

Gun violence has become a defining feature of American society, leaving a trail of death and devastation in its wake. Mass shootings and school shootings have become all too common, and the debate over gun control has reached a fever pitch. While many Americans view guns as an essential part of their culture and identity, others see them as a dangerous and deadly force that needs to be controlled. As we explore the issue of gun violence in America, it is important to consider the various factors that contribute to this problem and the potential solutions that can help save lives.

A Recent spike in violence

The Covid-19 pandemic has brought about an unexpected correlation to the spike in gun incidents in the United States. With the rise of Covid-19 cases, many individuals were left feeling isolated and anxious. This, coupled with political and social unrest, has contributed to an increase in gun-related incidents. The statistics surrounding gun violence in the United States can be alarming. With each passing year, the number of incidents and deaths related to gun violence continues to rise. In 2020 and 2021, these numbers reached new heights, leaving many wondering what can be done to address this critical issue.

In 2020, there were 43,569 total incidents and 38,680 deaths related to gun violence, an increase of 5% and 15% respectively from 2019. In 2021, there were 61,585 total incidents and 40,028 deaths related to gun violence, an increase of 41% and 4% respectively from 2020.

Suicide

Suicide rates in the United States have been on the rise in recent years, and it is becoming an increasingly pressing issue. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), suicide was the tenth leading cause of death in the United States in 2019, with over 47,500 individuals taking their own lives. The COVID-19 pandemic has only heightened concerns about the mental health and well-being of individuals across the country, and suicide prevention efforts are more important than ever.

In 2021 the American Indian / Alaskan Native population of the US was approxiamately 9.7 million. This population exhibits the highest suicide rates and in 2021 the male AI/AN population was estimated to be 42.6 people per 100,000 or 0.0426%.

Approximately 52.8% of all suicides in the US are commited via the use of a firearm.

More than half of all firearm-related deaths (54%) are caused by suicides, and the majority of all suicides (53%) involve a firearm. Despite mass shootings often receiving more media attention, firearm suicides are more prevalent than homicides. The data from 2020 shows that 54% of all firearm-related deaths were suicides, 43% were homicides, and 2% were accidental discharges or undetermined causes. This marks a small decrease from 2018 and 2019 when firearm suicides accounted for over 60% of all firearm deaths during that period. Further analysis of suicides shows that guns were involved in 53% of all suicides in 2020, making them the primary method for suicide.
